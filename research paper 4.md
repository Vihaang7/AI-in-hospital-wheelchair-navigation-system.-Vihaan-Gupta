# Paper 2 — Notes

**Author and Title**
Suling Yang, Alan K. Mackworth — "Hierarchical Shortest Pathfinding Applied to
Route-Planning for Wheelchair Users"

**URL to the Research Paper**
https://www.cs.ubc.ca/~mack/Publications/FICCDAT07.pdf
(Publisher record / DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-540-72665-4_46)

**Publication Record**
In Z. Kobti & D. Wu (Eds.), *Advances in Artificial Intelligence*, Canadian AI 2007,
Lecture Notes in Computer Science, Vol. 4509. Springer, Berlin, Heidelberg, 2007.
DOI: 10.1007/978-3-540-72665-4_46.

**Key Result or Finding**
Introduces the Hierarchical Shortest Path (HSP) algorithm and a hybrid of HSP and A*
(HSPA*), which find optimal (not just near-optimal) solutions in logarithmic time on
multi-level maps, versus the exponential blow-up of classical Dijkstra/A* as map levels
increase. Demonstrated on synthetic hierarchical maps (campuses → buildings → floors →
rooms) and applied in a real wheelchair route planner covering three UBC buildings.

**Gap, Limitation, or Future Work Identified by the Authors**
The running-time analysis was based on artificial (synthetic) examples rather than the
full real-world UBC campus. The authors call for testing on more actual, larger-scale
maps; tuning the two HSPA* parameters (α and β) using stochastic local search rather than
hand-picking them; and further comparison of HSP/HSPA* against other hierarchical
pathfinding algorithms.

**Relevance to Your Project**
This is the closest academic precedent to HospitalNavigator's `_k_astar_paths` method.
It shows that structuring a map hierarchically (building → floor → room — matching the
project's own `Floor` attribute on each location) can make A*-based wheelchair routing
scale far better than a flat graph, which is exactly what the current single-graph
implementation doesn't yet exploit as the map grows.

**Technical Approach or Methodology**
A theoretical-plus-empirical algorithms paper. The map is decomposed into a hierarchy of
clusters (e.g. campus → building → floor → room), each level abstracted into a smaller
graph of "border nodes" connecting clusters. The Hierarchical Shortest Path (HSP)
algorithm precomputes shortest paths between border nodes at each level; HSPA* combines
this hierarchical precomputation with an A* search refined at query time, using two
tunable parameters (α, β) to trade off precomputation cost against query-time optimality.
The authors prove HSPA* returns truly optimal paths (unlike prior near-optimal
hierarchical methods like HPA*/PRA*) and empirically time it against classical Dijkstra/A*
on synthetic multi-level maps, then wire it into a working wheelchair route-planner UI
tested on real UBC building maps.

**Dataset URL(s)**
No public dataset. The empirical timing tests used artificial/synthetic hierarchical
maps generated by the authors, and the applied route-planner demo used real UBC campus
building maps (ICICS, X building, Dempster Pavilion) that were not published as a
downloadable dataset.
